Billionaire Robert Earl Holding's daughter Anne appeals order to pay staggering $200 MILLION in divo

Oil and ski resort heiress Anne Holding is appealing a court order to fork out the most expensive divorce payout in Wyoming history to her estranged husband of 40 years.

Holding is arguing before the Wyoming Supreme Court that a lower court wrongly ordered her to pay more than $202 million to her husband Christian Peterson, which she claims wrongfully violates her family inheritance.

The 67-year-old is one of three children of Mormon billionaires Robert Earl and Carol Holding who built or operated multiple companies including Little America and Sinclair Oil (now REH Co.).

Peterson, who shared four children with Holding, filed for divorce on November 23 2021 and the pair hashed out their financial disagreements at a bench trial in October 2022. 

At the time of the divorce Peterson's net worth was about $6.15 million while his wife's net worth was about $602 million, which did not include the roughly $482 million in assets in a trust that Holding controlled.

According to court documents obtained by Cowboy State Daily, Holding was ordered to pay $202,581,208, which represents half of the stock her family gave to her throughout the marriage and half of another smaller investment.

Billionaire Mormons Robert Earl (right) and Carol Holding's (left) daughter Anne has appealed a court order to pay a staggering $200 million divorce settlement to her ex-husband of 40 years Christian Peterson

The Sinclair Oil heiress argued to the Wyoming Supreme Court that District Judge Suzannah Robinson was wrong to consider the wife's family inheritance as part of the property to be divided.

She claims there is no proof that Peterson needed the money to settle debts or handle poor health circumstances and therefore argues he should not have access to that inheritance. 

Peterson countered that she should have raised this while the divorce proceedings were taking shape in the lower court.

'This Court should not consider it for the first time on appeal,' Peterson's brief obtained by the outlet reads.

District Judge Suzannah Robinson ordered Holding to pay $202,581,208, which represents half of the stock her family gave to her throughout the marriage and half of another smaller investment

He also pointed to Wyoming case law giving district courts 'considerable discretion' when splitting up property in divorces.

Peterson noted that there is no requirement in Wyoming's statutes to split up inheritances due to economic need and said 'this is contrary to public policy.'

Holding doesn't have access to $202.6 million in cash and would have to sell shares in order to pay off the divorce settlement, she argued.

In the divorce proceedings Robinson specifically avoided giving half of Holding's shares to Peterson so as not to disadvantage her next to her siblings who would own much more stock in the family business had she done so.

Holding claims that while this contingency was issued the court 'did indirectly, what it refused to do directly.'

Peterson has disputed Holding's claim that she would have to sell off assets and argued that she would be able to get a loan for the settlement.

The amount is much larger than Holding had first proposed to give Peterson in the early phases of the divorce - which was either $1million per year or a lump sum of $10 million.

'Although $10 million would be viewed as wildly fortunate to most Americans and Wyoming divorce litigants, this is not the question before the court,' Robinson wrote at the time.

'The question before the court is what is fair and equitable in this divorce with these parties.'

Peterson claimed that Holding had been squirreling away shares to a trust that their daughter started, implying that she'd committed fraud.

He said a fraud finding wouldn't be necessary to hold up the court-ordered payout. 

One transfer allegedly happened after Holding met with a divorce attorney in 2014, court documents obtained by the outlet claim. 

Another happened immediately after the pair's official separation in 2018.

Holding noted that Robinson made no findings of fraud in her decision letter which described the assets split, arguing that she should not have considered the value of that trust when diving up the divorce property.

Peterson had initially tried to get that trust added into the divorce but failed.

'Those assets are not [Holding's] property,' Holding's brief read. 'They are third-party property. Under Wyoming law, the District Court could not account for and divide the value of those assets, absent a finding of fraud.'

Robinson had found that Holding's activity with the trust bore some 'badges of fraud,' however, which pushed the division in Peterson's favor without an actual fraud finding.

Holding only has access to about $21 million in cash she told the district court in the earlier filing.

Despite this, Robinson ordered Holding to pay the more than $202 million by June 28 and after that she would owe Peterson an interest rate of 10 percent which amounts to $55,502 per day. 

By the end of the appeal Peterson will be owed $3.1 million in interest. 

Holding has asked the court instead to let her pay off the divorce payment over the course of the next 10 years, saying that she shouldn't have to pay millions just because she decided to appeal the outcome.

Peterson argued against the idea saying inflation would affect what he was owed, while Holding would get to invest and profit from the lump sum while he only got a portion of it each month. 

'Wife's request for 10 years of interest-free payments again demonstrates that Wife wants to be able to continue to assert control over Husband,' Peterson argued.

Robinson denied Holding's request for a longer timeline and the Sinclair Oil heiress appealed the high court May 8. The Wyoming Supreme Court is yet to make a decision on the matter.

It remains unclear why the pair who share four children divorced but in the previous court hearing it's claimed they both had thoughts of divorce for years prior to their official separation in 2018.

The Holding family are behind storied ski resorts Sun Valley and Snowbasin and also own hotels, nearly 400,000 acres of land and Sinclair Oil a privately held company that drills for oil and gas and operates refineries and pipelines.

The fortune was created by Holding's father Robert Earl who started out managing the Little America motel in Wyoming in the 1950s. 

After he died aged 86, his wife Carol and three children took over the business. Their family is worth $2.5 billion according to Forbes.

Robert Earl Holding died in 2013 after complications of a stroke he suffered in 2002, a senior vice president at Sinclair Companies told the New York Times at the time.

Little America's success came from the 24 hour service station's remote location and few other places to rest and refuel for motorists and truckers. 

The service station soon became a chain of 'travel plazas' with its growth providing capital for Robert Earl to explore other businesses.

In a 2000 interview with a ski magazine Robert Earl said of the early business: 'Old U.S. Highway 30, which ran in front of it, was not much more than a trail. 

'You could stay for five dollars per night, hamburgers were 35 cents, ice cream cones were a nickel, gasoline was 16 cents a gallon.'

As the Holding empire grew Robert Earl bough a Mobil refinery in Casper Wyoming in the 1960s, followed by Sinclair Oil in 1976. 

The company now has more than 2,700 service stations mostly located in the West and Midwest.

Just one year later Carol Holding, who was her husband's frequent business partner, raised the potential sale of the Sun Valley ski resort which had fallen into disrepair since the glory days as a getaway for Ernest Hemingway. 

Neither had ever skied before but from this purchase the Holding ski business grew, with the pair buying Snowbasin near Salt Lake City in 1984.

Robert Earl also spent more than $185 million on the 775-room Gran America Hotel in Salt Lake City. Across the street from the luxurious hotel was the Little America Hotel which boasted 850 rooms.

The 'visionary and hard working builder of empires' was remembered by family and friends April 2013 at the Federal Heights Latter Day Saints Ward in Salt Lake City.

According to Deseret News three of his 12 grandchildren told heartfelt stories that 'brought tear and smiles' to those mourning.
